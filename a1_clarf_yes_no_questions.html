Yes/No Questions

How to ask questions that answer "yes" or "no" — with be and with other verbs.

●  question ●  negative ●  positive
A yes/no question puts a verb (be) or an auxiliary (do/does) before the subject.
The answer is short: Yes, ... or No, ....
Questions with "be"
With be, there's no extra auxiliary — be itself moves to the front of the sentence.
be — am / is / are 🪪 identification 🎂 age 🌍 origin 😎 status 👤 description
Statement → Question
I am ready. → Am I ready?
She is tired. → Is she tired?
They are here. → Are they here?
Short answers
Am I ready? → Yes, I am. / No, I'm not.
Is she tired? → Yes, she is. No, she isn't.
Are they here? → Yes, they are. / No, they aren't.
Questions with other verbs
With other verbs (work, like, play...), we add Do or Does before the subject. The main verb goes back to its base form — no -s, even for he/she/it.
do / does + base verb 🚗 transportation 🍽️ meals 💍 belongings ⚽ activities
Statement → Question
You work here. → Do you work here?
He works here. → Does he work here? (3rd person singular: do → does)
They like coffee. → Do they like coffee?
Short answers
Do you work here? → Yes, I do. / No, I don't.
Does he work here? → Yes, he does. / No, he doesn't.
Do they like coffee? → Yes, they do. / No, they don't.
Side by side
Is she a teacher? Yes, she is.
Does she teach English? Yes, she does.
Is he busy today? No, he isn't.
Does he work on Sundays? No, he doesn't.
Common mistake
✗ Does she works here? — wrong, don't add -s twice.
✓ Does she work here? — the -s only goes on does, not on the main verb.
