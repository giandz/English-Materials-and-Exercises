Adverbs of Degree

How strong or weak a quality is — from not enough, to just right, to too much.

Two different jobs: most of these words simply show intensity (very, quite, fairly...). But too and not enough are special — they don't just mean "a lot" or "a little," they mean more or less than what's needed, and usually signal a problem.
The scale
too 🟥🟥🟥🟥🟥🟥🟥🟥 excess / problem
extremely 🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩⬛ very strong
very 🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩⬛⬛ strong
quite 🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩⬛⬛⬛ above average
fairly 🟩🟩🟩🟩⬛⬛⬛⬛ moderate
somewhat 🟩🟩🟩⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛ mild
slightly 🟩🟩⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛ weak
barely 🟩⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛ almost not at all
not enough 🟥⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛ insufficient / problem
Examples
too🟥🟥🟥🟥🟥🟥🟥🟥

This coffee is too hot — I can't drink it yet.

The exam was too difficult for most students.

extremely🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩⬛

She's extremely talented at painting.

It was extremely cold last winter.

very🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩⬛⬛

He's very tired after the trip.

This soup is very spicy.

quite🟩🟩🟩🟩🟩⬛⬛⬛

The movie was quite good, actually.

I'm quite sure I locked the door.

fairly🟩🟩🟩🟩⬛⬛⬛⬛

The instructions were fairly clear.

It's fairly common to feel nervous before an interview.

somewhat🟩🟩🟩⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛

The results were somewhat surprising.

I'm somewhat familiar with the software.

slightly🟩🟩⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛

The room was slightly messy when I arrived.

This shirt is slightly too big for me.

barely🟩⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛

I could barely hear him over the noise.

We barely made it to the train on time.

not enough🟥⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛

There isn't enough light in this room to read.

He wasn't experienced enough for the job.

Word order
Most degree adverbs go before the adjective: very tired, quite good, extremely cold.
Enough is the exception — it goes after the adjective: tall enough, not "enough tall". For the negative, use not + adjective + enough: not tall enough.
